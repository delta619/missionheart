What is CHD?

What is CHD?
CHD is the presence of structural defects in the heart from the day the baby is born. This arises from failure of normal embryological development resulting in abnormal communications (holes) in the heart, abnormal connections, narrowing of valves and arteries and various other anomalies.

What is the incidence of CHD in India?

What is the incidence of CHD in India?
There are various estimates of the incidence of CHD depending on which studies you refer to but on an average the incidence in India approximates 9 per 1000 live births, which approximates to annual incidence of over 200 thousand babies borne every year with CHD. This places a significant burden on the already stretched resources currently available to cope with such problems.

How do parents recognize this condition?

How do parents recognize this condition?
There is a wide spectrum of presentation of CHD ranging from absent or minimal symptoms to the other extreme of babies coming to the doctors in a critical and life threatening state with severe heart failure or with very low oxygenation and bluish discoloration (also called cyanosis). Babies with mild CHD may escape detection in childhood by both parents and doctors as they may not cause symptoms and the clinical examination may be passed off as normal. These patients are usually detected later in life sometimes incidentally. At the other end of the spectrum are those who present on the first day of life neonatal period with critical life-threatening disease in whom urgent medical intervention is required to save the babies life. For the vast majority the age of presentation falls somewhere between these two extremes.

The symptoms of the babies usually fall in one of two groups

Group 1:   Includes babies who present with severe breathlessness, rapid respiratory effort or with feeding difficulty (breast feeding is the metabolically most demanding activity a baby performs). They manifest a typical suck-feed-suck cycle where they let go of the mothers breast multiple times in the feeding cycle during a single feed. This results in the baby being continuously hungry with irritability and incessant crying. Some mothers also notice increase perspiration on the babies forehead. As these problems increase the baby fails to gain weight and has delayed physical milestones. In addition, flooding of the lung by these defects causes increase frequency of lower respiratory tract infection and recurrent pneumonias. If the baby does not present in the neonatal period i.e. the first month of life, the most common presentation is after 6 weeks to one year of age. This type of presentation is typical of babies with large ‘holes” and flooding of the lung, abnormal severe obstruction of blood flow from the left ventricle, severely deformed and small left ventricle, intrauterine maternal infections which can affect the babies heart muscle pumping or rarely severe abnormalities of heart rhythm which can affect the babies heart function. The more complex and severe the anatomic defect, the earlier in life is the presentation. For smaller defects and those which do not place a burden on the babies heart it may be brought to the attention of the doctor in a regular visit where the physician may hear an abnormal sound in the babies’ heart called a murmur.

Group 2:   Some parents find that their babies have an abnormal bluish discoloration (called cyanosis). As in previous group, those who present at birth, usually have a severe cardiac problem necessitating immediate medical attention and often a life saving surgery. Those with milder problems present later on in life maybe in the first or second year with progressively increasing discoloration. Some babies especially in the first year of life can experience life threatening spells where they become intensely blue, with severe grunting and respiratory efforts, altered sensorium and loss of consciousness. These are referred to as cyanotic spells and are a medical emergency. If they are not diagnosed earlier in life then the parents often complain of progressive increase in breathlessness relieved by squatting. This group of condition is usually referred to as “blue babies” are usually caused when there is a large communication between the pumping chambers in addition to a severe obstruction of blood flow to the lungs. This results in the blue (deoxygenated) blood bypassing the lungs where it is supposed to go for oxygenation, and instead going via the hole to the left side of the heart to cause the blueness. This is also seen in babies whose right side of the heart is not developed at all (Hypoplastic right heart) and in those whom the blood circulation is running in parallel circuits and not as is normal in a series - a condition called transposition of great arteries. These later 2 conditions usually present at birth and the baby is very sick.

What should one do if a child has suspected CHD?

What should one do if a child has suspected CHD?
The first thing to remember is that the diagnosis of CHD does not necessarily mean that the baby will need surgery. In fact, a significant number of cases turn out to be mild defects where all that is required is watchful waiting. Oftentimes some of the smaller defects heal spontaneously by themselves. Despite this, it is a must that the child be referred to a specialist at the earliest to assess and diagnose the child’s problem and chart a management plan for the future. Those babies who present early in infancy usually have with serious heart defects requiring immediate intervention at birth or in the first year of life. Once referred to a pediatric cardiologist /center the doctor will assess the baby in detail with special attention to the weight, overall general condition will check the saturation using pulse oximeter and also listen to the child ‘s heart beat with a stethoscope for abnormal sounds. He may often ask for an ECG and chest Xray to be performed. But the most important test to assess the baby’s heart is the 2D ECHO CARDIOGRAPHY WITH COLOR DOPPLER STUDY. THIS TEST IS AN ABSOLUTE MUST with any child with suspected CHD.

What is 2DEcho cardiography and color doppler?

What is 2DEcho cardiography and color doppler?
Essentially this is an ultrasound examination of the heart performed in a careful and systemic manner. Addition of color doppler to the study enables the doctor to assess the severity of any of the structural defects that may be present. It is essential for the baby to be quiet during examination and hence the baby should be well fed and comfortable just prior to the test. In a few babies the doctor may resort to giving a mild oral sedative which is extremely safe and enables complete examination to be done. This test has no side effects whatsoever and is the key to comprehensive diagnosis which enables the doctor to correctly advise regarding management options. It is to be remembered that such tests should be carried out by specialized doctors or centers who have vast experience in dealing with such babies as sometimes making a correct diagnosis can be tricky. Once the basic tests are performed the doctor will be in a better position to advise the parents as to the correct course of further action.
